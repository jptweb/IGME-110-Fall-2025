## IV. Part 1: User Flow Diagrams (The Planning Phase)

### What is a User Flow?
A user flow maps out the journey a person takes through your app to accomplish a specific goal. It's like giving someone directions from point A to point B, but for navigating your app.

### Connection to Last Week
Remember from [10B UI/UX Design Process](10B.md#iii-uiux-design-process-overview)? User flows are **Step #1** - before you design screens, you need to understand what people are trying to do.

### Why Seven Flows?
You have three different user personas from Deliverable #1:
- **Primary user** (3 flows) - your main target audience
- **Power user** (2 flows) - someone who uses the app extensively  
- **Casual user** (2 flows) - someone who uses it occasionally

**Each persona has different goals and needs different paths through your app.** This balanced breakdown ensures you think meaningfully about all three user types without overwhelming any single persona.

### What Makes a Good User Flow Diagram?

Your user flow diagrams need four components:

1. **Title** that explains the user action
   - Example: *"Change shock intensity"*
   
2. **Entry point** where the user starts
   - Example: *"App Home Screen"*
   
3. **Series of steps** the user takes
   - Example: *"Find Settings Button" ‚û°Ô∏è "Navigate to Settings Screen" ‚û°Ô∏è "Change Intensity to Desired Level"*
   
4. **End success state** where they finish
   - Example: *"Confirm Change"*

### Visual Example
For our purposes, a simplified version like this is fine:

![screenshot](../documents/_images/user-flow-diagram.png)

### Format Options
Your flows can be:
- **Actual diagrams** (arrows, boxes, flowchart style)
- **Easy-to-read formatted text** (like the example above)

Both are acceptable - choose what works best for your team!

### Real-World Note
**In professional UX work**, user flows are often more detailed and include:
- Decision points ("if user clicks X, then show Y")
- Error states ("what happens if login fails?")
- Multiple paths ("logged in users see A, new users see B")

For this project, simplified high-level flows are fine - we're focusing on the core happy path.

### What Are Lo-Fi Mockups?
Lo-Fi (low-fidelity) mockups are simple wireframes showing:
- **Layout:** Where things are positioned
- **Content:** What information appears
- **Basic functionality:** What's clickable

**What they're NOT:**
- ‚ùå Final colors, fonts, or branding
- ‚ùå Detailed graphics or photos
- ‚ùå Pixel-perfect designs

**Think:** Sketches with boxes and labels, NOT polished designs like your infographics

### Why Lo-Fi First?
It's faster to:
- Test if your layout makes sense
- Rearrange elements
- Discover missing features
- Get feedback on the core idea

**Before** investing time in making it look pretty!

### How Many Screens?
**Minimum of FIVE screens** that demonstrate your app's core functionality.

**Pro tip:** Look at your user flows - which screens appear most frequently across multiple flows? Start with those.

### Paper vs. Digital?
**Your choice!**
- **Paper mockups:** Fast to create, easy to iterate in real-time
  - Example: [Mobile Application Design : Paper Prototype Video (1:16)](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=y20E3qBmHpg)
- **Digital wireframes:** Can use Canva, Figma, Photoshop, or similar
  - Easier to share/submit
  - Can copy/paste elements

### Connection to Testing
**These screens are what you'll test next class!** Other students will:
- Look at your screens
- Try to complete tasks using your mockups
- Give you feedback on what works and what doesn't

## VI. Part 3: Testing Script with 5 Tasks (The Preparation Phase)

### What is a Testing Script?
A testing script guides you through the process of having someone test your prototype. It includes:
- Tasks for the user to attempt
- Questions to ask
- Space to record observations

### The Template
Use the [provided testing template (script)](../documents/p3-wireframe-feedback-script.md) - it's already structured for you!

### Creating Your 5 Tasks

**Step 1:** Choose 5 of your 7 user flows that you want to test

**Strategy tip:** Select tasks that cover all three personas - for example, 2-3 from primary user flows, 1-2 from power user, and 1 from casual user. This ensures you test across different user experiences.

**Step 2:** Turn each flow into a task instruction

**Example transformation:**
- **User Flow:** "Find a Quick Dinner Recipe"
- **Task for testing:** "You want to make dinner in 30 minutes or less using only 5 ingredients. Use the app to find a recipe."

**Good tasks:**
- Give context ("You want to...")
- State a goal ("...find a recipe")
- Don't give away the solution ("Click the search button")

**Bad tasks:**
- Too vague: "Use the app"
- Too specific: "Click Home, then Search, then Filter"

### Why Only 5 Tasks?
You have 7 flows total, but:
- Testing takes time
- You want feedback on your CORE features first
- You can always test more later

Choose the 5 most important flows that represent the heart of your app, making sure to test across all three personas.

## VIII. Why Are We Doing All This? (The Big Picture)

### Connecting to Study Guide 11
[Study Guide 11: User Testing](https://docs.google.com/document/d/1f5h_8OBTGzORzFFOK5QamQqt4XgW4_Xe_ERmpZ9bmU0/edit?usp=sharing) covers why user testing matters.

**Key reasons:**
- **You are not your user** - what makes sense to you might confuse others
- **Catch problems early** - easier to fix a sketch than a finished product
- **Validate your ideas** - are you solving the right problem?
- **Discover missing features** - users will try to do things you didn't anticipate
